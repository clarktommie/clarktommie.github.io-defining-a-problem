Link to Dataset: Diabetes patients Uncleaned Data

One of the most common questions in diabetes research is how Body Mass Index (BMI) relates to the likelihood of having diabetes. We know that excess body weight can be a risk factor for developing diabetes, but what’s the exact connection? Does a higher BMI correlate with a higher likelihood of being diabetic?

By understanding the relationship between BMI and diabetes risk, this project can provide valuable insights that can be used for early detection and prevention of diabetes. The visualizations will help identify high-risk individuals, making it easier to target preventative measures, such as lifestyle changes or medical interventions, at an earlier stage, potentially reducing the burden of diabetes on healthcare systems and improving public health outcomes.

To explore this question, we'll visualize the relationship between BMI and AGE, with different colors representing the diabetes status (Diabetic vs. Non-Diabetic). By plotting this, we can see if there’s any noticeable pattern between BMI and the likelihood of having diabetes across different age groups.

Additionally, we can further break down this relationship by using histograms to compare the BMI distribution between diabetic and non-diabetic individuals. This will help us understand whether people with higher BMI are more likely to be diabetic, or if other factors come into play.

Handling Missing Values: Why Dropping Rows Makes Sense¶

After checking for missing values in our dataset, we can see that only a few entries are incomplete. Specifically, columns like AGE, Urea, and HbA1c have a small number of missing values—ranging from 1 to 3 missing entries in each column.

Given that such a small percentage of the data is missing (just a few rows across several columns), dropping the rows with missing values is a reasonable choice. This is especially true because:

  1. Minimal Data Loss: Since only a small number of rows contain missing values, removing them won’t significantly impact the size or quality of the dataset. We’re not losing a large amount of data, and the remaining dataset is still robust enough for analysis.

  2. Simplicity: Dropping missing values is a quick and straightforward approach, which is often preferred when the missing data is minimal. It avoids the complexities of imputation or filling missing values, which could introduce bias if not handled carefully.

  3. Preserving Data Integrity: By removing rows with missing values, we’re ensuring that every entry in the dataset is complete, which is especially important when preparing the data for analysis or modeling. This way, we avoid potential issues that could arise from using incomplete rows.

After dropping these rows, we can see from the output that there are no missing values left in the dataset. This leaves us with a clean, fully populated dataset, ready to move on to the next steps in our analysis.

Tidying Up the Dataset for Easier Analysis¶

As we dive deeper into our analysis, we realize that our dataset could use a little tidying up. We have some columns that could be combined, and a few that could use more intuitive names. So, we’re going to clean things up a bit to make everything more manageable.

First, we notice that we have two columns representing the female gender—one with an uppercase "F" and the other with a lowercase "f." We don't need both, so we’re going to merge them into a single column called Female.

Next, we have a couple of columns that show whether a person is diabetic or not, but they’re spread across two columns each—one for non-diabetic status and another for diabetic status. To simplify, we’re combining them into just two columns: NonDiabetic and Diabetic. This will make it so much easier to work with going forward.

We also want to make the column names more intuitive. The column that shows male gender will be renamed to just Male, and the column that represents those in the pre-diabetic stage will be renamed to PreDiabetes.

By the end of this process, our dataset will be much cleaner, with all the information we need neatly organized and ready for deeper analysis.
